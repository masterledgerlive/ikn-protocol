## 🌟 The Story That Starts Here

This is not just a technical document. It is the next chapter of a story that started in a physics lab in Switzerland in 1989, written by a quiet Englishman who wanted to solve his own filing problem.

His name is Sir Timothy John Berners-Lee. Most people have never seen his face. Every single one of them has used his invention today.

---

> **TimBL** — Born June 8, 1955 in London. His parents were both mathematicians who worked on one of the first commercially built computers ever made. As a child, Tim learned electronics by tinkering with his model railway. At Oxford he built his own computer from a second-hand television and a soldering iron. He graduated with a first-class degree in physics with no particular plan for his future.

---

He bounced between programming jobs. Nothing stuck. Then in 1984 he took a consulting position at CERN — the European particle physics laboratory in Geneva. The same lab, as you will see, that appears later in IKN's Phase 3.

---

> **TimBL** — At CERN, every researcher used different computers, different documentation systems. Nobody could find anything. If you needed to know something, you had to know who to ask. Tim kept getting asked the same questions. He got tired of answering them. So he built a system.

---

On March 12, 1989, he handed his supervisor Mike Sendall a proposal for a global information management system. Sendall read it and scrawled four words across the top: *"Vague, but exciting."*

---

> **TimBL** — Tim's response to that story, decades later: *"Thank goodness it wasn't 'Exciting but vague.'"*

---

By November 1990 he had built the first web browser, the first web server, and the first website — which described what the World Wide Web was and how to use it. He published it on December 20, 1990.

Then he gave it to the world. No patent. No company. No fee. He simply released the specification as an open standard and walked away from a fortune that by any reasonable calculation should have been his.

---

> **TimBL** — He invented the thing that made Google possible. The thing that made Amazon possible. The thing that made Facebook possible. His net worth is $50 million. Bill Gates — who built his empire on top of Tim's web — is worth $130 billion. Tim has never expressed a single regret about giving it away. A colleague once said: *"He can be the life of the party, without making the party about him."*

---

He watched what happened next. The open web he had built, designed for everyone, slowly became the property of three corporations. Google. Amazon. Facebook. The data — your data, the whole map of human thought and behavior — was harvested, monetized, and weaponized. The tool he built to connect humanity was turned into a surveillance machine.

---

> **TimBL** — *"The power of the web wasn't taken or stolen. We collectively, by the billions, gave it away with every signed user agreement and intimate moment shared with technology."* — Tim Berners-Lee, 2018

---

So in 2016, at age 61, after already having changed the world once, he started over. He called his new project **Solid**. The mission: give the data back.

That is the story IKN is the next chapter of. We carry his thread. We finish what he started. This document is dedicated to him.

## Solid: The Missing Layer Tim Already Built

When Tim watched Google and Facebook take the open web and wall it off, he did not give up. In 2016 he launched **Solid** — a technical specification for giving every human being their own personal data pod. The idea: your data does not live inside Facebook's servers. It lives in YOUR pod, hosted wherever you choose. Apps request access. You say yes or no. You can revoke it at any time.

---

> **Solid Protocol v0.11.0** — The official spec at solidproject.org/TR/protocol opens with this: *"The aims of the Solid project are in line with those of the Web itself: empowerment towards an equitable, informed and interconnected society. Solid adds to existing Web standards to realise a space where individuals can maintain their autonomy, control their data and privacy, and choose applications and services to fulfil their needs."* — This is the mission statement IKN was born to extend.

---

The five technical pillars Solid lays down:

| Pillar | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **1: Data Pods** | Personal Online Data Stores — hosted anywhere the user chooses. Your pod travels with you across platforms, providers, and lifetimes. The spec defines a storage resource as the root container for all a user's data, accessible via standard HTTP with fine-grained Web Access Control (WAC). |
| **2: WebID** | A universal identity standard Tim invented — not owned by any platform. Your WebID is a URI that resolves to a profile document describing who you are and where your pod lives. One identity. Every app. No platform lock-in. |
| **3: Linked Data / RDF** | All data in a Solid pod is stored as RDF — the triple structure Tim built in 1999. Subject → Predicate → Object. Any app that speaks RDF can read any pod without being specifically engineered for it. This is the interoperability layer. |
| **4: Access Control** | The spec defines Web Access Control (WAC) and Access Control Policy (ACP) — fine-grained permissions per resource per agent. You grant read, write, append, or control access. You revoke it with a switch. *"Solid will not only allow you to control who gets access to data, but you can also turn off that access,"* Tim said. |
| **5: Open Standards** | The entire Solid ecosystem is built on W3C standards: HTTP, RDF, OWL, SPARQL, Solid-OIDC for authentication. No proprietary formats. No vendor lock-in. The spec itself is open and forkable at github.com/solid/specification. |

Solid is alive, active, and growing. The spec is currently at version 0.11.0. Meetings are held every Wednesday at 14:00 UTC. Tim is still co-editing it at age 69.
